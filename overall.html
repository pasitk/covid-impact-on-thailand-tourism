Thailand's devasted tourism sector faces uncertain recovery
The number of tourist arrivals around the world in the first half of 2020 has dropped 65% from the previous year according to the World Tourism Organization (UNWTO), after international travel almost vanished because of the Coronavirus pandemic. This is part of the 2020 economic loss that is three times that of the global economic crisis that began in 2007. A country which has necessarily been affected by this calamity is Thailand, where the tourism industry is one of the most important sectors of the economy. Businesses and people in this industry are suffering.
"I was a photographer for a tourism guide company, and I used to travel in a lot of provinces in Thailand. However, when the COVID-19 outbreak began, I had no job for 3 months. My company may be out of business now I think." says Pornpon Thanasuwannatarn who lost his job during the pandemic period, and then became a food delivery driver and restaurant owner to earn a living.
He adds that the sign of danger appeared during Chinese New Year period, or around last week of January, "From that time, many tour guides started to talk or post on their Facebook profile, asked that what should they do if the lockdown period would come. Some of them started selling foods, returning to their hometowns, or change their careers permanently."
His income fell from at least $160 per week to only $32-$64 per month. He tells us that his savings are running out, and he is going to sell goods at the market from August.
His story is just one of many in this difficult time. There are also other opinions from the people in the tourism industry in this article, which I interviewed them on the phone.
In the big picture, Thailand is popular among tourists around the world because of its beautiful nature, cultural attractions, and the famous Thai food. In 2019, the receipt from international and Thai visitors was around $96 billion, or 17.83% of Thailand's gross domestic product in 2019.
Kittipong Rueanthip, Senior Economist at Krungthai COMPASS research centre in Thailand, says the importance of tourism in Thailand has increased dramatically in the last 10-20 years. "At the starting of the 2000s, it was around 3-6%. However, if we look just only receipts from international tourists nowadays, it is around 10% of GDP, so it has increased a lot."
"There are more than three million employees who work in tourism-related sectors, from 40 million workers. So, the tourism-related sectors are huge and very important to employment status." Kittipong adds.
Tourism receipts dropped dramatically, with difficult recovery
The COVID-19 pandemic has damaged all sectors of Thailand's economic system, including tourism. The warning signs started when China, the biggest source of international tourists for Thailand, suffered from an outbreak in the last months of 2019. This was followed by the announcement to ban Chinese people from booking overseas tours in January 2020. The number of foreign visitors fell from that month on and hit zero in April, with a ban on commercial flights and the borders closed as part of a state of emergency. Meanwhile, the number of Thai visitors fell dramatically as well. (The term 'visitors' includes both tourists and excursionists)
This means the income from these visitors vanished. Total receipts from all visitors in the first nine months of 2020 fell to less than half of what they were in the same period of 2019.
It seems like the tourism sectors in Thailand are now in a recovery period nowadays. There had been only a few new domestic infected people in these recent months, and lockdown measures were eased to a point at which everything is almost back to normal.
Nevertheless, people in the tourism industry don't expect a quick recovery.
"Our forecast indicates that receipts from Thai tourists this year will be $20.80 billion, falling from $34.56 billion in the previous year," says Kitti Pornsivakij, the assistant president of the Tourism Council of Thailand, as a representative of the tourism-related private sector. "As far as I heard from the entrepreneurs, the door is now closed for the hope to gain the number of foreign tourists in this year."
The Tourism Council of Thailand also predicts that the revenue from foreign tourists will be only $19.52 billion, down from the previous goal of $64 billion. That means total receipts from tourism in 2020 will be only 41% of what they were in 2019.
Meanwhile, the public sector expects a little less revenue than the forecast of the private sector.
"We forecasted that it will recover to around 30%, both in terms of the number of domestic tourists and the receipts from them", says Watcharaphan Bunnag, the marketing officer at the Tourism Authority of Thailand, "We expect that the number of international tourists will recover to not more than 10%." He believes social distancing measures will decrease the capacity of transportation systems and increase the cost. Travellers who cannot afford to pay this higher fee will change their destination to other routes to save money.
The ban on international commercial flights to Thailand is another critical factor in Watcharaphan's analysis. There will be a limited number of foreigners allowed to enter the country for specific reasons, such as business travellers. Even though he believes that international commercial flights will be allowed to land again from October, he also thinks that there will be only some foreign countries in which their citizens will be allowed. The Thai government will accept only the citizens from the countries where the outbreak situations are safe enough. The concept of 'Travel Bubble', or the agreement which one destination country will permit travellers from certain selected countries to enter its area, may be introduced as well. He also adds that although the international flight arrivals will be allowed, the travellers will be only the people who have a demonstrated need to travel.
"They will not be the people who say 'Yeah... let's go to Paris!', or 'Hey, I wanna go to the USA tomorrow'", Watcharaphan maintains, "They will rethink again about the severity of the outbreak in their destination areas. In the past, when we traveled, we use our hearts more than our brains. But from now on, we have to use our brains before our hearts."
His prediction is quite similar to the latest government's response in September. The Thai government decided to allow international tourists to enter the country under special types of visa called 'Special Tourist Visa', aimed to attract long-stay tourists. These tourists will be permitted to stay in Thailand for 90 days, but this period can be extended up to not more than 270 days. Some of foreigners in other groups such as investors, businesspeople, people who have work permits, teachers, and students are allowed to enter the country as well.
In the longer-term, Kitti says domestic tourism in 2021 will rebound to nearly the same level as 2019. The revenue from domestic tourists is expected to reach $28.8 billion. At the same time, the numbers of international tourists will depend on the progress to find an effective vaccine for the new coronavirus. If the vaccine can be found in the first quarter of 2021, the number of foreign tourists in the next year may reach 30 million people or equals $48 billion receipt.
"Nevertheless, as some experts expected, if we find the vaccine in the third quarter, the number of foreign tourists will only be 20 million people and income will be only $32 billion or half of the income we got in 2019." Kitti explains.
It's also a tragedy for the businesses
All related businesses have been affected in this challenging time, to varying degrees. A survey from the Tourism Council of Thailand revealed that entertainment venues were completely shut down, followed by spa and sightseeing businesses which were almost all closed (98% and 91% respectively). Overall, 65% of all tourism-related business was temporarily shut down. That means the same percentage of employees in these businesses, or about 2.6 million people, were temporarily unemployed. The survey results also revealed that 8% of employers in tourism-related businesses plan to dismiss their employees in the third quarter of 2020, while another 30% are hesitating about employee dismissal.
Meanwhile, the official figures for unemployed people who had ever worked from the office of national statistics have been released as well. The only group of industries in these figures which can be directly connected to tourism is 'Accommodation and Food Service'. The number of unemployed persons who had ever worked in this industry group shockingly increased more than four times from the first to the second quarter of 2020. We also found that the benefits of many employees, such as overtime, food, and other income have gone.
Kittipong explains that laying off employees in these sectors at this time may increase the level of inequalities because the employees may not regularly receive high income. Meanwhile, the entrepreneurs have been affected as well because the hotel or restaurant businesses are not 'high-margin' businesses because of high competition. This may lead to a cash flow problem, and the companies can be collapsed easily.
We have also reported on some interesting effects on people and businesses in several essential industries: accommodation, food delivery platforms, the aviation industry, and the tour operator sector.
Different opinions about the support:
Entrepreneurs' demand VS Economist's concern
The Thai government has launched several measures to support entrepreneurs at this challenging time. These include soft loans, loan repayment holidays, and deferral of tax payments. Meanwhile, the social security fund will pay benefits for employees who are covered and who were dismissed, temporarily laid off, or who resigned. If employees are not covered by the social security fund, they can request a particular benefit from the government. A survey result from The Tourism Council of Thailand has revealed that 86% of business owners and employees in tourism-related businesses received help from government measures.
However, the council has raised concerns about uncertainties in the labour market. Many business owners still have not decided if they will lay off their employees, so the council has asked the government to extend the period of supportive measures. For example, extending the loan payment holiday until the end of 2020, or making the criteria for soft loans approval more flexible so more businesses can take advantage of them. Kitti reveals that the private sector is negotiating with the Bank of Thailand about these criteria. He also proposes that the government should set up other funds with more specific purposes to support tourism-related businesses. The funds can provide loans which are easy to access or provide grants to improve tourism marketing and employees' skills.
In June, the cabinet approved two main packages, worth three-quarters of a billion dollars, to stimulate domestic tourism. The first package ('We travel together') was designed to subsidise accommodation fees, transportation fees, and other travelling expense for Thai travellers. It also helped accommodation owners, transportation operators and other entrepreneurs such as restaurant or tourist attraction owners. Another package ('Encouragement') was designed to subsidise the tour service fee for public health volunteers in small local hospitals all over the country. The government hopes that this package will encourage these volunteers and help travel agency businesses to survive.
Kitti, as a representative of the private sector in the tourism industry, says he is quite pleased with all of these packages. However, the only thing he feels disappointed with is the size of the budget the government provided for each package.
Kittipong believes that these two main packages will 'keep businesses alive' because the budget in these packages is only part of a government loan. This loan, which is worth $12.8 billion and which was set up for social and economic rehabilitation, represents only 5-6% of GDP. He believes that it cannot compensate for the lost income from the decreasing number of foreign tourists. Nevertheless, he still thinks the measures will stimulate the local economy to some extent, along with other fiscal policies such as particular benefits for the employees.
However, he is concerned about using the budget in tourism rehabilitation. He emphasises that every business has to face some risks, and the government has to consider the taxpayer when it wants to spend. He adds that the people will lose confidence in the government if tax is used to subsidise businesses which then make profits that stay in the hands of the business owners.
"The government should do those things the private sectors cannot do. For example, if you are worried about an economic crisis, or that the unemployment rate will increase because the hotel businesses is collapsing, you can provide them with soft loans or debt repayment suspensions" says Kittipong.
Domestic tourism is a key
All of the interviewees agree that in this difficult time when numbers of international tourists in Thailand will remain lower than usual, domestic tourism is key to fostering the tourism industry. However, tourism promotion varies from one part of the country to the next. Watcharaphan suggests that every city should be promoted if they have potential by using only their own strengths. He insists that some cities may not be able to give tourists or customers everything.
"For example, when you talk about Phuket, it has a lot of potential in things like recreation, entertainment, or even meetings. But, if we talk about Krabi, the province is not really a meeting area." Watcharaphan explains. Kitti agrees that there are many destinations which are quite hard to help because they were designed to support foreign tourists more than Thai tourists.
However, Kitti believes that Thai travellers will play an important role to supporting tourism in the 'second-tier provinces', where the number of tourists is still low. The proportion of receipts from tourists supports this opinion. There are 66 out of 77 provinces in Thailand which made more money from Thai tourists than from foreign tourists in the first four months of 2019. This pattern is still the same in 2020, and 52 from these 66 provinces are second-tier provinces.
Like Kitti, Kittipong believes the areas where Thai tourists predominate will see a quicker recovery: the famous national park Khao Yai for example or Kanchanaburi province, where Thai people like to spend long holidays.
"If the number of tourists who visit those areas is close to the number before the outbreak period, their revenue might fall to some extent, but they still have the cash flow to operate their businesses. Hence, those areas will see a quicker recovery." says Kittipong.
He points to the mobility trends data from Apple Maps which revealed the change in driving route requests in each Thailand province. This data indicates that the number of driving route requests in provinces where Thai tourists predominate is sometimes higher than in the period before COVID-19. This suggests the tourism industry in those areas is going to recover.
Some examples of these provinces are Nakhon Nayok, Petchaburi, and Samut Songkram. It's worth noting that these provinces have famous tourist attractions, or are close to Bangkok. While the areas which are far from Bangkok and have a higher proportion of foreign tourists, such as Chiang Mai, Mae Hong Son, and Phuket, tend to see low numbers of direction requests. This is in line with his analysis, which indicates that people are going to travel shorter distances because the economic situation is still delicate.
On the other hand, Watcharaphan also mentions MICE (Meetings, Incentive Travel, Conventions, and Exhibitions) businesses. He says the MICE sector is another interesting choice which the public sector can use to support domestic tourism. In 2019, the domestic MICE industry generated $3.4 billion in revenue.
"The public sector has to set up these activities to maintain the economic status of each province. Fortunately, they already have their plans to set up these activities, and they are implementing these." says Watcharaphan.
He also emphasises that attempts to stimulate domestic tourism will help the industry to survive and will reduce the number of job losses, they will not guarantee total recovery for the tourism industry.
For international tourists, Kitti believes Thailand will have a greater proportion of tourists from East Asian countries such as China, Japan and South Korea as people opt to travel shorter distances. He expects more exciting tourism products and in-depth customer segmentation, which will lead to better marketing.
For him, Thailand should attract more tourists from other countries besides China, to reduce the risk when Chinese tourists are not able to travel or do not want to travel. However, Watcharaphan believes this is not the right time to promote Thailand's tourism very aggressively as the COVID-19 outbreak, continues in many countries.
The Thailand tourism confidence index report provides other suggestions to help the tourism industry and to attract foreign tourists in the future. These include rehabilitating tourist attractions and the environment, promoting wellness tourism and medical tourism which can attract high-paying customers, and creating an image of a safe and sterile place to travel. According to the estimates by the Global Wellness Institute (GWI) in 2017, Thailand was ranked 13th of the top wellness tourism destination markets -- something future tourists may well be looking for as the world pandemic begins to ease.
